generations without anyone stopping to ask why they worked.

PART ONE: THE WAKE-UP
CHAPTER 1
Your body is under siege. Not from a single enemy, but from a thousand small exposures that
accumulate day after day, year after year, until the systems that keep you healthy can no
longer keep up.
This isn’t fear-mongering. It’s biology. And understanding it is the first step toward reversing it.
The modern toxic load
Every day, the average woman is exposed to over two hundred synthetic chemicals before
she leaves the house. They’re in your skincare products, your cleaning supplies, the plastic
containers you store food in, the non-stick coating on your pans, the receipts you handle at
the store, and the pesticides on conventionally grown produce.
These aren’t just environmental nuisances. Many of them are endocrine
disruptors—chemicals that mimic or interfere with your body’s hormones. Bisphenol A in
plastics, phthalates in fragrances, parabens in cosmetics—they enter your body through
your skin, your lungs, and your digestive system, and they accumulate over time.
Add to this the Standard American Diet—processed foods loaded with refined sugar, seed
oils, artificial preservatives, and ingredients designed in a laboratory to make you eat more.
Your gut, which was designed to process whole foods, is now trying to make sense of
chemical compounds it has never encountered in thousands of years of human evolution.

Then there’s stress. Not the acute stress your body was designed to handle—the kind that
sends adrenaline surging so you can run from danger. Modern stress is chronic. It’s the
low-level hum of a constantly buzzing phone, an overflowing inbox, a never-ending to-do list,
and the pressure to be everything to everyone.
Chronic stress keeps your cortisol elevated all day and into the night. Elevated cortisol
disrupts your sleep. Disrupted sleep impairs your body’s ability to repair itself. And without
repair, inflammation builds.
The cascade
Here’s what happens when these exposures accumulate: your gut lining breaks down,
allowing particles into your bloodstream that don’t belong there. This triggers an immune
response—inflammation. Chronic inflammation disrupts your hormones. Disrupted hormones
slow your metabolism. A slower metabolism means weight gain, especially around the
midsection. And the oxidative stress from all of this accelerates aging at the cellular level.
It’s not one thing causing your symptoms. It’s a cascade. And the solution isn’t addressing
one symptom at a time. It’s resetting the entire system.
That’s what this book is for.
The good news? Your body already knows how to heal. You just need to stop overloading it
and start supporting it with the right inputs—the ones it was designed to receive.

When your body is overwhelmed, it doesn’t break down everywhere at once. It follows a
predictable pattern. Understanding this pattern is what makes the 30 Day Total Reset
effective—because we address each system in the right order.
1. Your Gut
Your digestive system is home to trillions of microorganisms—collectively called your
microbiome. These bacteria do far more than digest food. They produce neurotransmitters
like serotonin and dopamine. They regulate your immune response. They metabolize
hormones. They influence whether you gain weight or lose it.
When the microbiome is disrupted—by processed food, sugar, antibiotics, stress, or
environmental toxins—the balance shifts. Harmful bacteria proliferate. The gut lining, which is
only one cell thick, begins to break down. This allows food particles and toxins to pass into
your bloodstream, triggering widespread inflammation.
Seventy to eighty percent of your immune cells live in your gut. When your gut is
compromised, your entire immune system is compromised. This is why gut health is Pillar
One and Week 1 of the reset. Everything else depends on it.

2. Your Hormones
Hormones are your body’s chemical messengers. Cortisol tells you when to be alert and
when to rest. Estrogen and progesterone regulate your cycle, mood, bone density, and skin
health. Insulin controls how your body uses fuel. Thyroid hormones set the pace of your
metabolism.
When cortisol stays elevated—from chronic stress, poor sleep, or gut inflammation—it
disrupts the entire hormonal cascade. Your body prioritizes survival over everything else.
Progesterone drops. Estrogen dominance can develop. Insulin sensitivity decreases. Thyroid
function slows.
The symptoms? Weight gain you can’t explain. Anxiety and mood swings. Terrible sleep.
Irregular cycles. Thinning hair. And a deep, persistent fatigue that makes you feel decades
older than you are.
This is why hormones are Pillar Two and the focus of Week 2. Once your gut begins to heal,
your body can start rebalancing its hormonal signaling—especially when you give it the right
inputs.
3. Your Metabolism
Your metabolism isn’t just about how many calories you burn. It’s how efficiently your body
converts food into energy, manages blood sugar, and decides whether to store fat or burn it.
When your gut is inflamed and your hormones are disrupted, your metabolism slows down
as a protective mechanism. Your body thinks it’s under threat, so it holds onto energy
reserves—especially around the midsection, where fat can be quickly mobilized in an
emergency.

This is why calorie restriction and excessive exercise often fail. You’re fighting against a
system that believes it needs to conserve energy. Until you address the underlying
inflammation and hormonal disruption, your metabolism will stay in protection mode.
Week 3 introduces the strategies that signal safety to your body: stable blood sugar, gentle
intermittent fasting, and strategic movement. Your body will shift from fat-storing to
fat-burning because the environment has changed—not because you’re starving it into
submission.
4. Your Cellular Health
Everything happening at the gut, hormonal, and metabolic level also affects you at the
cellular level. Oxidative stress—caused by inflammation, toxin exposure, poor sleep, and
chronic stress—damages your cells and accelerates aging.
This shows up as premature wrinkles, dull skin, thinning hair, low energy, brain fog, and a
general sense that your body is declining faster than it should. You’re not imagining it.
Cellular aging is measurable, and it’s driven by the same factors this reset addresses.
Week 4 focuses on renewal: antioxidant-rich foods, sleep optimization, stress reduction, and
purification rituals that support your body’s natural repair processes. By this point, your gut is
healing, your hormones are stabilizing, your metabolism is shifting—and your cells can finally
begin to repair and regenerate.

“The Babylonian Talmud promotes the idea that God authorized humans
 to heal themselves using the natural world that He created.”
 
This single idea is the foundation of everything in this book. It’s not mysticism. It’s not folk
superstition. It’s a practical philosophy that has guided Jewish health practices for over two
thousand years—and modern science is now validating it in ways that would have
astonished our ancestors.
Jewish tradition has always understood something that the modern wellness industry is only
now rediscovering: that health is not just about what you eat or how much you exercise. It’s
about rhythm, rest, intention, community, and a deep respect for the body as something
worth protecting.
Kosher: The original elimination diet
The laws of kashrut—the Jewish dietary code—are often understood as purely religious rules.
But look at them through a modern wellness lens and something remarkable emerges.
Kashrut requires intentionality around food preparation. It separates certain food categories.
It demands awareness of what enters your body and how it was sourced. Many of the foods
restricted by kashrut happen to be among the most inflammatory and hardest to digest.

This isn’t to say kashrut was designed as a health protocol. But the principles—mindful
sourcing, intentional preparation, avoiding certain food combinations, and eating with
awareness—map remarkably well onto the elimination diets used by functional medicine
practitioners today. In Week 1, we’ll use these principles as a framework for clearing your
system.
Shabbat: The original nervous system reset
Every week, for twenty-five hours, observant Jews disconnect from work, technology,
commerce, and the relentless productivity of daily life. This isn’t just a religious observance.
It’s a neurological intervention.
Research published in 2025 found that people who observe regular weekly rest sleep better,
experience less fatigue during the week, and report improvements in mental well-being.
Other studies have shown that digital disconnection reduces cortisol, improves attention,
and strengthens relationships—all of which directly impact hormonal health.
Shabbat was the original digital detox, the original “tech sabbath,” the original nervous
system reset. And in Week 2, we’ll build a modern version of it into your life—whether or not
you observe Shabbat traditionally.
Fasting: The original intermittent fasting
Jewish tradition includes multiple annual fasts, from the twenty-five-hour Yom Kippur fast to
shorter dawn-to-dusk fasts throughout the year. Long before intermittent fasting became a
wellness trend, Jewish communities were practicing periodic fasting as a spiritual and
physical discipline.

Modern research confirms what these traditions intuited: giving the digestive system periodic
breaks improves insulin sensitivity, supports autophagy (cellular cleanup), reduces

women-friendly fasting protocol inspired by these ancient practices.
Mikvah: The original purification ritual
The mikvah—the Jewish ritual bath—has been used for spiritual and physical cleansing for
thousands of years. Immersion in living water was understood as a transformative act of
renewal, a way of marking transitions and releasing what no longer serves you.
In Week 4, we’ll adapt this tradition into a modern self-care ritual. Not as a religious practice,

therapeutic herbs, and quiet reflection to support your body’s repair processes during the
final phase of the reset.
Sh’mirat haguf: The duty to guard your body
Perhaps the most powerful concept of all is sh’mirat haguf—the religious imperative to take
care of your body and soul. In Jewish tradition, your body is not something to punish, deprive,
or push past its limits. It is something to protect, nourish, and honor. This philosophy reframes
everything about health and wellness. You don’t do this reset to punish yourself for how you
look. You do it to honor the body you’ve been given.
Your ancestors already had the blueprint. This reset is about reclaiming it.

PREP GUIDE
Success in this program starts before Day 1. Take one day to prepare your kitchen, your
pantry, and your mindset. This is not optional—it’s the difference between finishing strong
and falling off by Day 5.
Step 1: The Kitchen Cleanout
Remove or set aside the following for the next 30 days: refined sugar and artificial
sweeteners, processed snacks and packaged foods, seed oils (canola, soybean, corn,
sunflower, safflower), conventional dairy, gluten-containing grains, alcohol, and anything
with ingredients you can’t pronounce. You don’t have to throw them away. Put them in a box,

Step 2: The Pantry Restock
Your reset pantry should include: high-quality proteins (wild-caught fish, organic chicken,
pasture-raised eggs, grass-fed beef), healthy fats (olive oil, avocados, coconut oil, ghee, nuts
and seeds), vegetables (especially leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables, root vegetables),
fruits (berries, pomegranates, citrus, apples), fermented foods (sauerkraut, pickles, kimchi,
coconut yogurt), bone broth or the ingredients to make it, herbs and spices (turmeric, ginger,
garlic, cumin, cinnamon, rosemary), herbal teas (ginger, chamomile, peppermint, turmeric).
The companion Recipe and Ritual Guide has a complete week-by-week shopping list.
Step 3: The Baseline
Before you start, rate the following on a scale of 1 to 10: Energy, Sleep Quality, Digestion, Skin
Clarity, and Mood. Write these numbers down. You’ll revisit them at the end of each week.
Take a photo if you want. This isn’t about vanity—it’s about documentation. In thirty days,
you’ll want to see how far you’ve come.

WEEK 1: THE GUT CLEANSE

Clear the Path
Your gut didn’t break overnight, and it won’t heal overnight. But the first seven days of this
reset are designed to dramatically reduce the burden on your digestive system so it can
begin repairing itself.
This week, you’re doing two things simultaneously: removing the inputs that are damaging
your gut lining, and introducing the foods that help it heal.
What you’re removing and why
Gluten can trigger zonulin release, a protein that increases intestinal permeability—even in
people without celiac disease. Refined sugar feeds pathogenic bacteria and yeast in the gut.
Seed oils are highly inflammatory and disrupt the omega-3 to omega-6 ratio your body
needs for balanced immune function. Conventional dairy contains hormones, antibiotics,
and proteins that many women struggle to digest. Processed foods contain emulsifiers,
preservatives, and additives that research has linked to microbiome disruption.
Removing these for thirty days is not a permanent restriction. It’s a temporary reset that gives
your gut the breathing room it needs to heal. After Day 30, you’ll reintroduce foods one at a
time to identify your personal triggers.

What you’re introducing and why
From Day 1, you’ll add gut-healing staples to every single day:
Warm lemon water every morning. Before coffee, before food. Warm water stimulates

Garlic, liberally. In the Talmud, garlic was so central to Jewish life that Jews referred to
themselves as “garlic-eaters.” Modern research confirms garlic’s powerful antimicrobial,
anti-inflammatory, and prebiotic properties. Use it raw when possible—crushed and left to sit
for ten minutes before cooking to activate the beneficial compound allicin.

The morning ritual is designed to do three things: hydrate your body after eight hours of

between the two. When the vagus nerve is stimulated, your heart rate drops, your cortisol
decreases, and your digestive system begins to function more efficiently. This is the
gut-brain connection in action.

If Week 1 was about clearing the physical debris, Week 2 is about calming the chemical

Your hormones don’t operate independently. They’re an interconnected web, and when one

Cortisol is not the enemy. In healthy amounts, it wakes you up in the morning, keeps you alert

keeps cortisol elevated around the clock. Your body thinks it’s in constant danger, and it
responds accordingly: it stores fat, disrupts sleep, suppresses reproductive hormones,
increases anxiety, and accelerates aging.
This week, everything is designed to send your body one message: you are safe.
We do this through three channels: hormone-supporting nutrition, adaptogenic herbs, and
the Shabbat Reset—the single most powerful practice in this entire program.

Understanding the HPA Axis
The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis is the communication system between your
brain and your adrenal glands. When your brain perceives stress—any stress, whether
physical, emotional, or chemical—it signals your adrenal glands to release cortisol.
In acute stress, this is life-saving. In chronic stress, it’s life-degrading. Chronically elevated
cortisol suppresses progesterone production (your body literally steals the raw materials
meant for progesterone to make more cortisol instead). This creates estrogen dominance—a
condition linked to weight gain, mood swings, heavy periods, breast tenderness, and
increased cancer risk.
High cortisol also impairs insulin sensitivity, meaning your cells become resistant to the signal
that tells them to absorb glucose. The result: elevated blood sugar, increased fat storage,
and persistent cravings for sugar and carbs.
This is why stress management isn’t a luxury—it’s a hormonal intervention.

Intermittent fasting: The Jewish connection
Jewish tradition includes multiple annual fasts—from the twenty-five-hour Yom Kippur fast to
shorter dawn-to-dusk fasts throughout the year. These weren’t punishment. They were
understood as acts of purification, reset, and self-mastery—a way of reminding the body

Modern research has validated these intuitions. Giving the digestive system extended breaks
from food improves insulin sensitivity, activates autophagy (your body’s cellular cleanup
process), reduces inflammation, and shifts the body into a fat-burning state.
For this reset, we use a gentle protocol: the 14:10 window. Fast for fourteen hours, eat within a
ten-hour window. For most women, this means finishing dinner by seven in the evening and
eating breakfast at nine in the morning. You’re essentially just skipping late-night eating and
delaying breakfast slightly.
Important: extreme fasting is not recommended for women, as it can disrupt reproductive
hormones and increase cortisol. The 14:10 window is gentle enough to capture the metabolic
benefits without the hormonal risks. If you feel lightheaded, anxious, or significantly fatigued,
shorten your fasting window. This should feel manageable, not punishing.

The meal composition rule
Every meal and snack should contain three things: protein, fiber, and healthy fat. This triad
stabilizes blood sugar, prevents insulin spikes, keeps you full, and maintains steady energy.
A meal with only carbohydrates—even healthy ones—will spike your blood sugar and crash it
within two hours. A meal with protein, fiber, and fat releases glucose slowly and evenly. This is
the difference between a body that stores fat and a body that burns it.
Mindful eating as spiritual practice
In Jewish tradition, a bracha—a blessing—is recited before eating. This isn’t just a religious
formality. It’s a pause. A moment of awareness before food enters your body. Modern
research on mindful eating shows that this simple act—pausing before you eat—reduces
overeating, improves digestion, and increases satisfaction from meals.
You don’t need to recite a Hebrew blessing. But before each meal this week, pause. Look at
your food. Take one breath. Acknowledge that what you’re about to eat will become part of
your body. Then eat slowly, chew thoroughly, and put your fork down between bites.

Glossary of Jewish Wellness Terms
Besamim — Aromatic spices used during the Havdalah ceremony at the end of Shabbat.

preparation methods.

Shabbat — The Jewish Sabbath, observed from sundown Friday to sundown Saturday. A day
of rest from work, commerce, and technology.
Sh’mirat Haguf — Literally “guarding the body.” The Jewish religious imperative to take care
of one’s physical health as a spiritual duty.
The Seven Species — The seven agricultural products listed in the Torah as special products
of the Land of Israel: wheat, barley, grapes, figs, pomegranates, olives, and dates.
